HI-MAPF - Towards Resource-Efficient Multi-Agent Deployment

Bharath Muppasani, Risha Patel, Biplav Srivastava, Vignesh Narayanan
University of South Carolina, USA
Paper Video Code

Abstract

Multi-Agent Path Finding (MAPF) is central to coordinating multiple agents in shared or contested environments. Existing algorithmic solutions to MAPF impose significant requirements on communication, sensing, and computation, limiting their applicability in resource-constrained robotic deployments. For instance, centralized search methods require access to global state information and high-bandwidth channels, while distributed learning-based methods depend on sophisticated onboard perception (e.g., LiDAR, RGB-D cameras) and frequent inter-agent communication. Consequently, these methods incur high hardware, computational, and communication costs that form a critical bottleneck for long-term robotic deployment.

To address these limitations, we formulate information-centric MAPF (I-MAPF) and introduce HI-MAPF, a hybrid framework that augments decentralized planning with a lightweight centralized coordinator. We further introduce a method-agnostic metric, Information Units (IU), to quantify coordination overhead as a proxy for deployment cost.

We evaluate HI-MAPF against recent communication-focused algorithms in both simulation — across unstructured and structured environments with 8 to 128 agents — and on ground robots in a physical deployment, demonstrating 2× to 510× reduction in information sharing while maintaining high success rates. Our results show that in controlled environments, HI-MAPF eliminates the need for sophisticated onboard perception, expensive compute, and frequent inter-agent communication, enabling coordination through minimal event-triggered alerts.

I-MAPF Formulation

We characterize MAPF coordination by the information available to each agent i at time t:

\(\mathcal{I}_i(t) = \bigl(\mathcal{M}_i,\, \mathcal{S}_i,\, \mathcal{O}_j,\, \mathcal{E}_{ij}(t)\bigr)\)

  • \(\mathcal{M}_i\): Map knowledge — bounded or global environment details.
  • \(\mathcal{S}_i\): Self-state information — start and goal locations.
  • \(\mathcal{O}_j\): Other-agent information — sensed or communicated.
  • \(\mathcal{E}_{ij}(t)\): Event-triggered coordination signals — predicted collisions or alerts.

In our proposed HI-MAPF framework, we minimize the total information budget. \(\mathcal{M}_i\) is a one-time broadcast, \(\mathcal{O}_j\) is strictly limited to localized alerts, and \(\mathcal{E}_{ij}(t)\) consists of minimal, event-triggered collision cells or short snippets—represented as Information Units (IUs).

HI-MAPF Framework

The HI-MAPF framework operates by minimizing the continuous exchange of information. Agents plan from local information, escalating only on detected conflicts, and returning to independent motion once the bottleneck clears.

Local planning first

Each robot plans from a local observation window without broadcasting global trajectories.

Conflict-triggered alerts

The coordinator intervenes only when a specific vertex or edge conflict is predicted.

Minimal information release

Escalation exposes only the cells or time-indexed path fragments needed by the active repair.

Bounded coupled planning

Joint search is reserved for residual bottlenecks that cheaper local repairs cannot break.

The key design choice is not the presence of a coordinator, but the fact that the coordinator reveals very little. Information Units (IU) makes that tradeoff measurable across algorithms and across environments.

Hardware Validation

We validated HI-MAPF on a team of 5 TurtleBot4 robots operating in a 6×6 physical grid using ROS 2. Robots communicated only via the lightweight alert protocol — no global map was broadcast; they relied on local odometry and sparse conflict alerts.
